The Forgeotten VideoDiscs

by Jaeden Laffey

video format capacitance videodiscs, similar to laserdiscs, built and played like a record.

Introduction

    video format war & invention of the videodisc

    During the 1980’s there was a video format war primarily between BetaMax and VHS (among others).
    VHS won, companies tried to find new innovative video formats and ways to keep revenue coming in after VHS.
    This introduced record-style videodiscs into the competitive video market.

Capacitance VideoDiscs

    Capacitance Videodiscs are a forgotten video format technology
    There are 2 different types of capacitance videodiscs
    RCA's Capacitance Electronic Disc and JVC's Video High Density
    RCA's Capacitance Electronic Disc (CED)
    JVC's Video High Density (VHD)

Behind the VideoDisc

  • VideoDiscs & Vinyl Records
    • The manufacturers of Capacitance VideoDiscs took direct influence from the vinyl record.
    The aim was to take the familiar disc style method of listening to music, and applying it to a new medium to newer and more cost efficient product for users

Creation of the Capacitance VideoDisc

    Pressing Vinyl records was very cheap to do.
    CED/VHD players cost less to build than VCRs, and included less "precision" parts.
    Chose to practice cost cutting methods, instead of embracing new technologies and advancements.

About Capacitance

  • “Capacitance = the ability of two adjacent conductors to store electric charge"
    • Used the method of Capacitance to encode video and audio
    “Capacitance = the ability of two adjacent conductors to store electric charge"
    in the CED/VHD these adjacent conductors are the carbon-loaded disc and the titanium electrode deposited on the diamond stylus.”
    VHD and CED have been the only 2 media formats which implement the use of capacitance to store

About the tech: CED

  • 25cm in diameter. 30min of video on each side
    • The CED was a record-style disc, 25cm in diameter, while traditional records were 30cm.
    2 sided, 30 min of video on each side, which was not enough to efficiently store a movie on one disc.
    Available from 1981-86 from RCA

About the tech: VHD

  • 25cm in diameter, up to 1 hour of video on each side
    • The VHD was a record-style disc, also 25cm in diameter
    2 sided, up to 60 min of video on each side
    Unlike the CED, the VHD was upgraded from Mono and could play Stereo audio.

Product Release

    The VHD was demoed by JVC in 1978
    Despite this, it was not released until 5 years later in 1983*
    The VHD first launched in Japan, and later in Europe, but the product never reached North America.

Using the technology

Discs kept in protective "caddys" so that users don't accidentally scratch or smudge the disc
Insert caddy to player, the machine takes the disc and returns the empty caddy
Diamond stylus read the discs electronically, unlike a regular record where the stylus reads the disc analoguely through the grooves

Alternative histories

    The technology was available and demoed in 1978
    However they were not released until 1981(CED) and 1983(VHD)
    If was released at the time of the demo, it would be released in the same year as the LaserDisc,
    allowing for greater customer adoption and CED/VHD could have been the next proprietary video format

Alternative histories

    If the CED/VHD had become more popular, flaws could be fixed and improvements could be made
    Could build upon the technology to create new media formats.
    Could have changed future technologies in the way LaserDisc did with CDs,DVDs

From our present day view:

    Not just a failed technology/format
    Provides an us with alternative method of solving the problem of file storage
    by using capacitance to electronically encode media for our enjoyment

From our present day view:

Gives us insight to alternative technologies
Forgotten technology that could be repurposed for different uses

Cultural context

    The VHD or CED never gained traction in North America
    Very successful in Japan for many years
    Still in use today, popular as collectors items in Japan
